Ballet has its origins in 15th century Italy. It was brought to France sometime between the 15th and 16th century when Catherine de Medici of Italy married the French King Henry II. Until 1681 the dances consisted of only men, as women were not allowed to dance in public at the time.

Early styles of ballet were based on location; French style, Russian style, and Italian style. The 19th century, or the romantic era, brought romantic style ballet. This era brought the iconic white tutu, pointe work and plots meant to bring the audience intense emotions.

Image Credit

Classical style ballet comes next and is indicated by related technqiues. What may be the most well known ballets, The Nutcracker and Swan Lake, fall into this style.

Neoclassical style goes against the grain. It is not strict in plot, set, or costumes. It is less formal and focuses on athletics rather than delicate precision.

Contemporary ballet is the last one. It takes from all other styles of ballet and dance. It combines acting and miming with the dance, and allows for plenty of experimentation.

Quick Facts

  1. Not every ballet dancer is a ballerina. The title of ballerina is reserved for principle dancers.

  2. Every pair of pointe shoes is handmade.

  3. Dancers will likely go through multiple pairs of pointe shoes in a week. Some companies will go through 15,000 a year.

  4. A single tutu can take 90 hours to make and cost around $3000.

  5. Due to the intricacies of the costumes, they cannot be washed. In most cases, they are hung up and sprayed with a freshening spray.

  6. Each dancer does their own hair and makeup. It takes a collective 400 hours for all of the perfect ballet buns to be done.

  7. When a dancer jumps en pointe, the tip of their big toe carries three times their body weight.

  8. A male ballet dancer may lift about one-and-a-half tons of ballerinas in a performance.

  9. A study showed that an audience can feel up to 60% happier upon seeing a tutu on stage.
